## True Answer

The Aharonov-Bohm (AB) effect is a quantum mechanical phenomenon that occurs when a charged particle, such as an electron, is subjected to a magnetic field in a region where it does not experience any classical force. In the context of a double-slit experiment, this means that the magnetic field is confined to the region between the slits and does not directly affect the electron's motion.

When a magnetic field is applied using the Aharonov-Bohm effect, the interference pattern of the electron passing through the double-slit experiment changes due to the phase shift experienced by the electron's wavefunction. This phase shift is a result of the electron's interaction with the vector potential of the magnetic field, even though the electron does not experience any classical force.

The change in the interference pattern can be described by the AB phase, which is given by:

Δϕ = (e/ħ) ∮ A·dl

where e is the electron charge, ħ is the reduced Planck constant, A is the vector potential, and the integral is taken over the path of the electron between the slits.

The AB phase causes a shift in the interference pattern on the screen, which in turn affects the probability of detecting an electron in a specific location. The probability distribution of detecting an electron on the screen is given by the square of the amplitude of the electron's wavefunction. Due to the phase shift, the maxima and minima of the interference pattern will shift, altering the probability distribution.

In summary, applying a magnetic field using the Aharonov-Bohm effect in a double-slit experiment with electrons will cause a change in the interference pattern due to the phase shift experienced by the electron's wavefunction. ### Introduction: The Core Principle

The Aharonov-Bohm effect is a cornerstone of quantum physics that demonstrates that the electromagnetic vector potential, $\vec{A}$, is not just a mathematical convenience (as it can be in classical electrodynamics) but a physically real and fundamental quantity. ### Step 1: The Standard Double-Slit Experiment (No Magnetic Field)

Let's establish the baseline for an electron passing through a double-slit apparatus without any magnetic field.

1.  **Wavefunction and Superposition:** An electron leaving the source is described by a wavefunction, $\psi$. When it encounters the two slits, its state becomes a superposition of passing through slit 1 and slit 2. The total wavefunction at a point `x` on the detection screen is the sum of the wavefunctions from each path:
    $
    \psi_{\text{total}}(x) = \psi_1(x) + \psi_2(x)
    $
    where $\psi_1$ is the wavefunction for the path through slit 1 and $\psi_2$ is for the path through slit 2.

2.  **Probability and Interference:** The probability, $P(x)$, of detecting the electron at position `x` is given by the squared magnitude of the total wavefunction:
    $
    P(x) = |\psi_{\text{total}}(x)|^2 = |\psi_1(x) + \psi_2(x)|^2
    $
    $
    P(x) = (\psi_1^* + \psi_2^*)(\psi_1 + \psi_2) = |\psi_1|^2 + |\psi_2|^2 + \psi_1^*\psi_2 + \psi_2^*\psi_1
    $
    The terms $|\psi_1|^2$ and $|\psi_2|^2$ are the probabilities of detection if only slit 1 or slit 2 were open, respectively. The crucial part is the **interference term**: $\psi_1^*\psi_2 + \psi_2^*\psi_1 = 2 \text{Re}(\psi_1^*\psi_2)$.

3.  **Phase Difference:** Let's express the wavefunctions in terms of their amplitude and phase: $\psi_1 = A_1 e^{i\phi_1}$ and $\psi_2 = A_2 e^{i\phi_2}$. The interference term depends on the phase difference, $\Delta\phi = \phi_2 - \phi_1$. This phase difference arises from the difference in the path lengths from each slit to the point `x` on the screen.
    The probability becomes:
    $
    P(x) = A_1^2 + A_2^2 + 2A_1A_2 \cos(\phi_2 - \phi_1)
    $
    This cosine term is what creates the characteristic interference pattern of bright fringes (constructive interference, high probability) and dark fringes (destructive interference, low/zero probability).

### Step 2: Introducing the Magnetic Field (The Aharonov-Bohm Setup)

Now, we modify the experiment. A long, thin, and ideally infinite solenoid is placed between the two slits, perpendicular to the screen.

- **The Key Setup:** The magnetic field $\vec{B}$ is perfectly confined _inside_ the solenoid.
- **The Electron's Path:** The electron's paths (through slit 1 and slit 2) go _around_ the solenoid, never entering it.
- **Classical Expectation:** Since the electron travels only in regions where $\vec{B} = 0$, the Lorentz force $\vec{F} = q(\vec{v} \times \vec{B})$ is zero. Classically, there should be **no effect** on the electron's trajectory and thus no change to the interference pattern.

### Step 3: The Quantum Mechanical Phase Shift

In quantum mechanics, the presence of a magnetic field is described by the **magnetic vector potential** $\vec{A}$, defined by $\vec{B} = \nabla \times \vec{A}$. Crucially, while $\vec{B}$ can be zero outside the solenoid, $\vec{A}$ is not.

The phase of a charged particle's wavefunction is modified by the vector potential. When a particle with charge $q$ travels along a path $C$, its phase acquires an additional term given by:
$
\Delta\phi_{\text{magnetic}} = \frac{q}{\hbar} \int_C \vec{A} \cdot d\vec{l}
$
where $\hbar$ is the reduced Planck constant.

Let's apply this to our two paths:

- The wavefunction for the path through slit 1, $\psi_1$, acquires an additional phase:
  $\psi'_1 = \psi_1 \exp\left(i \frac{q}{\hbar} \int_{\text{path 1}} \vec{A} \cdot d\vec{l}\right)$
- The wavefunction for the path through slit 2, $\psi_2$, acquires a different additional phase:
  $\psi'_2 = \psi_2 \exp\left(i \frac{q}{\hbar} \int_{\text{path 2}} \vec{A} \cdot d\vec{l}\right)$

The **new phase difference** between the two paths at the screen is the original geometric phase difference plus a new magnetic phase difference, $\delta_{AB}$:
$
\delta_{AB} = \left(\frac{q}{\hbar} \int_{\text{path 2}} \vec{A} \cdot d\vec{l}\right) - \left(\frac{q}{\hbar} \int_{\text{path 1}} \vec{A} \cdot d\vec{l}\right)
$
$
\delta_{AB} = \frac{q}{\hbar} \left( \int_{\text{path 2}} \vec{A} \cdot d\vec{l} - \int_{\text{path 1}} \vec{A} \cdot d\vec{l} \right)
$

This difference of integrals along two paths can be rewritten as a single closed-loop integral. The loop starts at the source, goes through slit 2 to the screen, and returns from the screen through slit 1 to the source.
$
\delta_{AB} = \frac{q}{\hbar} \oint_{\text{loop}} \vec{A} \cdot d\vec{l}
$

Using Stokes' Theorem from vector calculus, we can relate this line integral of $\vec{A}$ to the magnetic flux, $\Phi_B$, passing through the surface enclosed by the loop:
$
\oint \vec{A} \cdot d\vec{l} = \int_{\text{Surface}} (\nabla \times \vec{A}) \cdot d\vec{S} = \int_{\text{Surface}} \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{S} = \Phi_B
$
The magnetic flux $\Phi_B$ is the total magnetic field passing through the area between the two electron paths.

Therefore, the phase shift due to the Aharonov-Bohm effect is:
$
\delta_{AB} = \frac{q \Phi_B}{\hbar}
$
For an electron, the charge is $q = -e$, so the phase shift is:
$
\delta_{AB} = -\frac{e \Phi_B}{\hbar}
$

### Final Answer: The Effects on the Interference Pattern and Probability

#### 1. Change in the Interference Pattern

The total phase difference between the two paths is now the sum of the geometric phase difference and the Aharonov-Bohm phase difference:
$
\Delta\phi_{\text{new}} = \Delta\phi_{\text{geometric}} + \delta_{AB} = \Delta\phi_{\text{geometric}} - \frac{e \Phi_B}{\hbar}
$
The interference pattern is governed by the cosine of this new total phase difference. The original pattern had maxima where $\cos(\Delta\phi_{\text{geometric}}) = 1$. The new pattern has maxima where $\cos(\Delta\phi_{\text{geometric}} - e\Phi_B/\hbar) = 1$.

This means the entire interference pattern—all the bright and dark fringes—is **shifted spatially** on the screen. The amount of the shift is directly proportional to the magnetic flux $\Phi_B$ enclosed by the solenoid.

- If the magnetic flux $\Phi_B$ is increased or decreased, the interference pattern will shift sideways on the screen.
- The spacing between the fringes does _not_ change, as this is determined by the electron's wavelength and the slit separation. Only the absolute position of the entire pattern is translated.
- Interestingly, if the flux is an integer multiple of the "flux quantum," $\Phi_0 = h/e$ (or $2\pi\hbar/e$), the phase shift $\delta_{AB}$ will be a multiple of $2\pi$. Since $\cos(\theta) = \cos(\theta + 2\pi n)$, the pattern will shift back to its original, no-field position.

#### 2. Change in Detection Probability at a Specific Location

The probability of detecting the electron at a fixed position `x` on the screen is now dependent on the magnetic flux $\Phi_B$. The probability function is:
$
P(x, \Phi_B) = A_1^2 + A_2^2 + 2A_1A_2 \cos\left(\Delta\phi_{\text{geometric}}(x) - \frac{e\Phi_B}{\hbar}\right)
$
Let's consider a point `x` that was originally a dark fringe (a minimum) in the absence of the magnetic field. This means the probability was near zero because $\cos(\Delta\phi_{\text{geometric}}(x)) = -1$.

By turning on and adjusting the magnetic field $\vec{B}$ (and thus the flux $\Phi_B$), we can change the value of the Aharonov-Bohm phase shift. If we adjust the flux such that $e\Phi_B/\hbar = \pi$, then the cosine term becomes $\cos(\Delta\phi_{\text{geometric}}(x) - \pi)$. Since $\cos(\theta-\pi) = -\cos(\theta)$, a point that was a minimum ($\cos=-1$) now becomes a maximum ($\cos=1$).

**In summary:**

- **The interference pattern shifts.** The positions of maxima and minima are translated sideways as a function of the magnetic flux $\Phi_B$.
- **The probability at a fixed point changes.** A location on the screen that initially had a low probability of detection can become a high-probability location, and vice versa, simply by varying the magnetic field inside the solenoid, even though the electron never comes into contact with that field. This demonstrates a non-local effect unique to quantum mechanics and underscores the physical reality of the vector potential $\vec{A}$.
